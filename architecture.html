Russian Church Architecture

The first stone constructions in Russia, particularly in ancient Rus' churches, exhibited a considerable influence of Greek Byzantine forms (in Kiev, now the capital of Ukraine). St. Sophia, established in the 11th century in Kiev.

St. Vladimir in Novgorod is an example of northern mediaeval (12th-century) architecture.

The architectural style of Moscow's St. Basil's Cathedral is the apex of Russian ornate church architecture.

Western Influence: St. Petersburg (17th & 18th c.)

The great architects of Russian secular architecture borrowed from Western European models. Bartolomeo Rastrelli created magnificent palaces in the Baroque style.

The second signature architectural style of St. Petersburg is the Neo-Classical, whose leading architect was Carlo Rossi. Across the Palace Square from the Winter Palace is Rossi's Staff Headquarters.

The Soviet Monumental Style and Communism

During the Soviet times (1917-1991), the government constructed massive structures in a monumental style to represent the state's power and Communism's eternal promise.

The most famous of these structures were created during Stalin's reign and continue to dominate Moscow's skyline.

The "tall buildings" were constructed in a "wedding cake" style, as seen in the Moscow State University built 1949-1953.

Contemporary Moscow

The heritage of gigantic, super-sized structures that defined city architecture in the twentieth century continues in post-Soviet Moscow.

Today, Moscow is erecting massive skyscrapers in a worldwide international style that can be observed across world capitals where the strength of international commerce is on show, rather than in a specific national design.
